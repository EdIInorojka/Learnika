# Business plan reference

 

## 1. Executive summary

 

Learnika is a B2C and B2B educational platform for children ages 3-18, parents, teachers, mentors and schools. It begins with mathematics for grades 7-9 in Russia and expands only after educational effectiveness, retention and unit economics are demonstrated.

 

The first product addresses five recurring needs:

 

1. identify and close knowledge gaps;

2. support the current school topic and textbook sequence;

3. help with real homework without giving the answer;

4. let the learner ask or explain a step through typed or confirmed voice input;

5. prepare for tests and OGE.

 

The durable differentiation is a unified knowledge graph combined with textbook mappings, analysis of solution steps, safe hints, transfer checks and school assessment workflows.

 

## 2. Mission and vision

 

### Mission

Make personalized educational support accessible to every child while preserving independent thinking and reducing repetitive work for parents and teachers.

 

### Vision

Become the digital learning layer connecting the child, family, textbook, school and examination system. The learner's knowledge history survives changes of class, textbook, school and learning format.

 

## 3. Primary problems

 

### Families

- grades do not reveal the root cause of mistakes;

- children often receive complete answers from search, answer sites or universal AI chats;

- parents spend time explaining homework or pay for frequent tutoring;

- generic courses do not match the selected textbook and school pace;

- exam preparation may start late and ignore prerequisites;

- parents lack a concise answer to what improved, what remains difficult and what should happen next.

 

### Teachers and schools

- creating variants, conducting, checking and analyzing work consumes time;

- paper results do not automatically become personalized remediation plans;

- equivalent variants and class-wide skill maps are difficult to produce quickly;

- schools require organizational isolation, roles, audit, security and procurement readiness.

 

## 4. Product model

 

The learner has a canonical skill profile independent of textbooks. Homework images, typed steps and learner-confirmed voice transcripts are input channels into the same educational workflow. Three tracks are blended into one weekly plan:

 

- school track: current class sequence and selected textbook;

- restorative track: prerequisite gaps found by diagnostics and attempts;

- target track: upcoming tests, VPR, OGE, EGE or advanced goals.

 

Every meaningful attempt updates evidence. Mastery changes require multiple signals, recency and transfer, not one correct answer.

 

## 5. Product portfolio ages 3-18

 

### Ages 3-6

Parent-led preschool product: speech, early literacy, counting, attention, memory and school readiness. Separate UX and methodological team are required.

 

### Grades 1-4

Foundational mathematics, Russian language, reading, homework routines, VPR preparation and parent-supported practice.

 

### Grades 5-6

Transition to subject-based schooling, mathematics, Russian and English, organization and closing prerequisite gaps.

 

### Grades 7-9

Initial production segment: mathematics, school support, homework, tests and OGE. Later add Russian, English, informatics and physics.

 

### Grades 10-11

EGE and university-oriented routes, profile subjects, advanced practice, written-response checking and admission planning.

 

### Cross-age products

Olympiad, advanced, summer intensive, career guidance and family subscriptions.

 

## 6. First market wedge

 

The main acquisition entry is multimodal homework support:

 

> Upload or photograph a mathematics homework task, then type or say what is unclear. The platform will identify where the learner is stuck and help them solve it independently.

 

This creates immediate value and repeat usage. Successful sessions lead to diagnostics, a personal plan and subscription.

 

## 7. Customer segments

 

### B2C

- families with children who have recurring homework difficulties;

- families already using tutors and seeking measurable efficiency;

- students preparing for OGE and EGE;

- high-performing students who want advanced study;

- families in regions with limited access to specialists.

 

### B2B

- individual teachers and tutors;

- private schools and educational centers;

- school networks;

- municipal and regional education systems after validation;

- publishers and content partners.

 

## 8. Revenue model

 

### B2C revenue

- self-study subscription;

- subscription with group mentor;

- extended plan with individual lessons;

- exam preparation plans;

- intensives before tests and exams;

- specialist checking of open responses.

 

### B2B revenue

- annual per-student or per-school licenses;

- teacher and assessment modules;

- implementation, training and support;

- premium analytics;

- partnerships and licensed content;

- API or white-label after platform maturity.

 

## 9. Indicative pricing hypotheses

 

Pricing is tested with real offers after a free diagnostic. Initial hypotheses:

 

- self-study, one subject: 790-1,290 RUB per month;

- up to three subjects later: 1,490-1,990 RUB per month;

- group mentor: 2,490-3,490 RUB per month;

- exam product: 3,490-4,990 RUB per month;

- individual lesson: 900-1,500 RUB;

- school license: determined by scale, support and procurement requirements.

 

These are test ranges, not promises.

 

## 10. Go-to-market

 

### B2C funnel

1. content or advertising around a specific pain;

2. free homework recognition or mini-diagnostic;

3. immediate useful hint and detected skill issue;

4. 3-7 day trial with several successful sessions;

5. subscription with transparent result and cancellation;

6. weekly report and repeat diagnostic;

7. family, teacher and specialist referrals.

 

### B2B sequence

1. pilots with individual teachers, clubs and private schools;

2. annual contracts with private networks and centers;

3. Russian software registry and security package;

4. applicable educational-material expertise;

5. municipal and regional trials with independent measurement;

6. public procurement and integrator partnerships after references exist.

 

## 11. Defensible assets

 

- canonical skill graph and prerequisite relationships;

- data about student steps, error types, effective hints and transfer;

- original reviewed content bank;

- mappings to federal programs, textbooks and exams;

- combined family and school workflows with explicit data boundaries;

- safe AI and voice-input method that supports thinking instead of replacing it;

- trust supported by measured learning outcomes.

 

## 12. Operating model

 

Content is produced through a controlled pipeline:

 

1. methodologist defines skill, prerequisites and mastery criteria;

2. author creates original explanations, exercises, solutions and hints;

3. mathematical editor checks correctness and edge cases;

4. pedagogical editor checks age, clarity and answer leakage;

5. QA generates variants and runs automated checks;

6. content is published as a version and reviewed from telemetry.

 

## 13. Organization

 

Early core team:

- frontend engineer;

- AI/math engineer;

- mathematics methodologist;

## 14. Financial planning assumptions

 

The business plan uses scenario ranges, not guaranteed forecasts.

 

- commercial year 1 begins after approximately 10-15 months of development and pilots;

- average paid users may grow from about 1,000 to 5,000, 18,000, 50,000 and 110,000 across five commercial years in the base scenario;

- B2B begins after product validation and may reach approximately 400,000 licensed learners by commercial year 5 in an accelerated scenario;

- gross margin improves through scale and AI cost control;

- accelerated six-year development may require approximately 500-850 million RUB in staged financing;

- a capital-efficient path remains longer in one subject and funds expansion from revenue;

- target break-even range is commercial year 3-5.

 

A separate spreadsheet model is required after price and pilot data exist.

 

## 15. Voice-input business hypothesis

 

Voice input is included as an optional convenience and accessibility channel, not as a separate voice tutor. The MVP supports short recordings, transcription, editable confirmation and temporary raw-audio retention. Success is measured through adoption, transcription correction rate, homework completion, transfer performance, cost per minute and privacy compliance. Realtime dialogue is gated until asynchronous voice input demonstrates educational and economic value.

 

## 16. Strategic gates

 

Expansion to a new subject or age band requires:

 

- validated demand;

- curriculum package and accountable method owner;

- original content and gold evaluation set;

- proven learning effectiveness;

- retention and support capacity;

- unit economics within approved limits;

- security and legal review;

- operational readiness.

 

School development requires an additional B2B gate with design partners, tenant-isolation evidence, procurement strategy and teacher workflow validation.

 

## 17. Long-term outcome

 

The platform should become a lifelong educational profile that understands what the learner knows, how they make mistakes, which explanation works and what next action is most effective—while remaining understandable to families and usable by teachers.
