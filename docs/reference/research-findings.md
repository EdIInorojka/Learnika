# Research findings

 

## 1. Primary parent survey

The initial survey included 24 parents. It is useful for qualitative direction but is not statistically representative of Russia.

 

### Sample limitations

- small sample size;

- geographic concentration, mainly Perm Krai and Kungur;

- most children were good-performing students;

- very limited representation of grades 5-7;

- about half of families were not currently paying for additional education.

 

### Strong signals

- 83% rated the concept 4 or 5 out of 5;

- only 42% said yes or rather yes to a paid subscription;

- 29% were undecided about payment;

- 88% preferred a format with a mentor or teacher involved;

- only one respondent chose fully independent learning;

- the most valued functions were detection of specific gaps and detailed error analysis;

- AI itself was not perceived as a primary benefit;

- parents wanted short weekly recommendations rather than complex dashboards;

- external game currency was not a primary reward preference;

- parents preferred rewards for improvement, correction of errors and successful transfer.

 

## 2. Product implications

1. Interest is higher than purchase intent; value must be demonstrated on the learner's own task.

2. The first experience should provide a concrete result within minutes.

3. The product should not be positioned as an AI tutor.

4. Human support remains important, but should be used efficiently and selectively.

5. Parent reports must answer four questions: what was learned, what is difficult, whether progress occurred and what happens next.

6. The product should reward mastery and independence, not time in the app.

7. Price must be tested through actual purchase offers, not only survey questions.

8. Input convenience must not weaken the no-answer learning model.

 

## 3. Voice-input hypothesis

Voice input was not directly validated in the first parent survey. It is therefore a product hypothesis, not a confirmed demand signal.

 

The hypothesis is that short voice questions and dictated solution steps will:

 

- reduce friction on mobile devices;

- help learners explain where they are stuck;

- increase completion of homework-helper sessions;

- improve accessibility;

- be especially useful for younger future age groups and language learning.

 

The hypothesis fails if voice has low repeated use, high correction rates, high processing cost, privacy concerns or no measurable improvement in completed learning sessions.

 

### Voice prototype tests

Test with learners:

 

- discoverability of the microphone button;

- willingness to speak near family or classmates;

- accuracy of Russian mathematical speech;

- whether transcript confirmation is understood;

- correction rate for fractions, signs, roots and powers;

- latency tolerance;

- fallback to text;

- effect on independent transfer;

- parent trust in temporary audio processing.

 

## 4. Next research program

### Quantitative study

Target at least 300 responses with quotas:

 

- grades 1-4: at least 75;

- grades 5-7: at least 75;

- grades 8-9: at least 75;

- grades 10-11: at least 75.

 

Split by geography, current tutoring use, spending level and academic performance.

 

### Interviews

Conduct 20-30 interviews with:

 

- parents paying for tutors;

- parents who stopped tutoring;

- families using free materials;

- exam-year families;

- learners separately from parents;

- teachers who regularly prepare and check tests.

 

### Prototype tests

Test:

 

- homework upload and first hint;

- typed versus voice question entry;

- whether learners understand the no-answer model;

- willingness to attempt before hints;

- trust in recognition, transcription and checking;

- parent report comprehension;

- preferred mentor format;

- price offers at several levels.

 

## 5. Evidence standard

A hypothesis becomes validated only when behavior supports it. Stated interest, clicks and praise are insufficient without repeated use, learning improvement or payment.
